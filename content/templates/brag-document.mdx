## What's inside

Each entry uses the same six fields — structured enough to scan in review season, detailed enough to defend under questions:

- **Initiative** — named clearly, with one line of business context. "Checkout v2 for annual plans," not "billing work."
- **Your role** — with a precise verb. *Led*, *drove*, *decided*, and *influenced* are not interchangeable, and the distinction protects your credibility in calibration.
- **Collaborators** — by name or function. PM wins are team wins; documenting them makes your entry more trustworthy.
- **Outcome** — a metric where possible, the strongest observable proxy where not.
- **Date** — quarter plus month. Undated entries become disconnected anecdotes.
- **What you said no to** — the most underused field. Deprioritization decisions show judgment better than most launches.

## How to use it

1. **Pick a cadence you can sustain.** A weekly five-minute habit beats a quarterly memory dump every time.
2. **Separate activity from impact.** For every entry ask: what changed because of this? Rewrite until the answer is clear.
3. **Add context within 48 hours.** Constraints, stakeholders, and rationale disappear fast and are hard to reconstruct.
4. **Attach the habit to a trigger.** The most common failure mode is stopping after three or four weeks because nothing prompts the update — tie it to Friday shutdown or weekly planning.

## When the doc isn't enough

A plain doc works well at first. It breaks when you need polished outputs quickly, need proof for collaborative wins, or stop updating because there's no habit loop.
